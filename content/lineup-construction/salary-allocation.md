# Salary Allocation

Salary allocation is the distribution of a fixed cap across roster spots. Using the entire cap can produce a strong lineup, but salary spent is not the same as points earned. The useful question is whether each salary choice supports the lineup's plan for the slate and contest.

## Reading Salary Cap Pressure

Cap pressure changes with pricing and available value. When several low-salary players receive secure roles, paying for multiple studs becomes reasonable because the cheap players offer credible volume. On a slate with weak value, forcing expensive names may create too many fragile roster spots. A balanced or mid-range build can preserve touches, targets, or minutes across the lineup.

Value plays should be evaluated by role, not by price alone. A backup running back moving into a starting workload has a different ceiling from a low-cost receiver who runs routes but earns few targets. The first can release enough salary for a stud without sacrificing much projection. The second may make the lineup look attractive while lowering its realistic scoring range.

### Leaving Salary on the Table

Unused salary is acceptable when it improves the lineup. This occurs most often in tournaments where a slightly cheaper combination carries similar projection, better correlation, or lower duplication risk. Leaving $300 has little strategic meaning by itself; leaving $1,500 requires a clear explanation supported by roles and ceiling.

Use this sequence:

1. Identify reliable value created by news or pricing.
2. Compare stud, mid-range, and balanced constructions.
3. Check whether the spending pattern supports [[stacking-and-correlation]].
4. Review popularity through [[ownership-and-leverage]].

Salary allocation should match slate context. Pricing efficiency, position scarcity, injuries, and the available game environments matter more than a fixed rule about spending every dollar.
