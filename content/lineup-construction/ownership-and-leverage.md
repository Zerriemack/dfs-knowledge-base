# Ownership and Leverage

Projected ownership estimates the percentage of lineups expected to roster a player. A heavily owned option is often called chalk. Chalk usually forms for defensible reasons: strong projection, favorable price, expanded role, or access to a high team total. The strategic question is not whether a player is popular, but whether the expected return supports that popularity in a specific contest.

## Thinking Beyond Raw Projection

Tournament results depend on score and relative position. A player projected for 22 points at 35 percent ownership can be a weaker tournament choice than a player projected for 19 points at 9 percent ownership if their ceilings are similar. When the popular player fails and the alternative succeeds, the lower-owned roster gains ground on a large share of the field.

Leverage can come from a direct pivot at the same position, a teammate whose success changes the chalk player's outcome, or a different game stack in the same salary range. A pivot should retain a plausible role and ceiling. Selecting an inferior player because the field dislikes him is not a useful wager.

### Duplication and Lineup-Level Ownership

Duplication risk rises when a lineup combines the most common value plays, obvious stacks, and full salary usage. Even a first-place score loses value when split among many identical entries. Reduce that risk with decisions that remain defensible:

- use a lower-owned player with comparable opportunity
- build a less common version of a strong [[stacking-and-correlation|stack]]
- leave salary unused when the cheaper combination projects similarly
- avoid pairing every popular value play in one lineup

Ownership belongs in the full construction process. [[contest-selection]] determines how aggressive the lineup should be. Small fields often reward modest pivots, while large fields demand a narrower and less duplicated path to first place. After lock, actual ownership can also shape decisions in the [[late-swap-process]].
